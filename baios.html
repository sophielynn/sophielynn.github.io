Design Brief

Ecology is important.

How can we get young people interested and involved?


Our environment is changing rapidly and at a grand scale. Tools for understanding and developing new knowledge about the environment are more important than ever, and experiential learning is a critical ingredient in environmental education. [NEEF 2015] When learners are active participants in their education, they perform better than they do in more passive learning environments.[Freeman 2014]

Business Idea

Current solutions fall short.


We can do better than the traditional methods for helping young people to explore ecology. Using these current tools, the process of discovery, research, creation, and sharing involves separate phases across several unintegrated platforms:

Tools for discovery and research

Textbooks lack the interactivity that users have come to expect from learning tools.

Documentaries may generate interest, but they facilitate mostly passive learning.

Online research quality is variable. The information may simply be the “computer screen” version of a textbook or a documentary.

A lecture proceeds in a linear fashion and has limited opportunity for participation.

Labs and field trips are limited by what is close by and affordable.

Tools for creating and sharing

A slide deck or report is a static document.

A science fair project limits ecological projects to materials and locations close at hand.

A Virtual Reality platform that facilitates the exploration of ecology and the connectedness of ecosystems.


Unlike current tools, BAIOS empowers explorers to chart their own journey and to browse author vivid content using compelling media and high-quality data and to share their own content with a global community—all in a single platform.

Explore an ecosystem in VR.

Using media from top-quality content providers like BBC, visit a whole new world. Use a gesture-based browsing system to investigate further without leaving the environment.

Learn from experts.

Experiment with data visualizations crafted by leading scientists to learn about how different factors in the ecosystem are connected.

Create your own visualization.

Select quantitative information related to the ecosystem and use data visualization software to build knowledge for yourself and maybe even discover something new.

Publish and share.

Save your work and publish to the global BAIOS library, where others can learn from your visualizations.

Explore the environment

By using different filters, users can observe and explore the ecosystem from different point of view. They can be more focused on the contents they are interested.

Collect the data

All data in the environment, including text, picture, chart, video, and 3D model, can be grabbed and collected to users’ inventory for further usage.

Create your own piece

With all the data collected, users can create their own artifacts in the VR environment. They could be posters, slides, video or even VR show rooms.

Roll-out Strategy


The Competition

BAIOS will hold an annual competition for middle- and high- school aged students who are interested in learning more about ecology. Students will respond to a challenge question related to a topic in ecology that’s important in current events with a visualization.

The competition winners receive scholarships to pursue STEM (science, technology, engineering, and mathematics) careers in college.

Scenario

Sonia is doing homework in her local library, where she encounters a BAIOS booth. There, she tries on a headset and browses a library of professionally designed explorations.

A few days later, Sonia sees a sign near her locker: her school has a BAIOS team! They are responding to this year’s BAIOS challenge: “What does 2 degrees really mean?”

Sonia joins the team and works with other students. They visit different ecosystems across the globe, experiment with data, and craft a data visualization for their submission.

A panel of judges reviews entries from all over the globe, and Sonia’s team is selected to win! Each team member receives a scholarship to pursue a science-related major at a prestigious university.
